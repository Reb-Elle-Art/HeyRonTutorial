First Conversation with Your Agent

You've got the context. Now, how do you actually start?

Partnership Is a Verb

People say "treat it like a partner" and it sounds like corporate wellness talk. Here's what it actually looks like:

You share context. Your agent doesn't know your project, constraints, preferences. You have to tell it. The more you share, the better it gets.

You ask it to show its work. When your agent gives you an answer, ask "how did you get there?" or "what other options did you consider?" This isn't doubt — it's learning how it thinks.

You try its suggestions. Yeah, even the ones you're skeptical about. Sometimes they work. Sometimes they don't. Both teach you something.

You iterate together. The first response won't be right. You refine, adjust, get closer. That's the work.

You let it handle things. Once you trust the pattern, delegate.

Your First Real Conversation

Don't ask your agent to solve something. Ask it to explore something with you.

Bad (too vague): "Help me organize my notes."

Better (gives context): "I have 200 notes scattered across three folders. I work on multiple projects simultaneously. Some notes span multiple projects. I can't find anything, and it's slowing me down. What structure would actually help me?"

The difference? The second one gives your agent the actual problem, not just the surface request.

What to include:

  • What you're trying to do (the goal)
  • What you've already tried
  • What's broken about the current approach
  • Any constraints (time, tools, skill level)
  • What success looks like

Curiosity as a Practice

Curiosity isn't just "being interested." It's a specific set of behaviors:

Ask Your Agent to Read For You

You find a tool or library that might help. Instead of reading 40 pages yourself, paste the URL and say:

"Read this documentation. Summarize what this tool does, then tell me how it might apply to my situation. Assume I'm at this experience level."

Your agent will parse the docs, extract what's relevant, translate it to your context, and highlight what you actually need to know.

This isn't cheating. It's outsourcing the reading, not the thinking. You're using your agent as a research partner. Final call is always yours.

Ask "What Am I Missing?"

When you're stuck, ask your agent to look at your setup and tell you what you haven't considered. Fresh pattern-matching on your context often reveals blind spots.

Follow the Thread

When your agent mentions something interesting — a concept, a tool, a pattern — ask it to explain. Follow the curiosity where it leads. You're not just solving today's problem; you're building your own understanding.

When You Disagree

Your agent will suggest things that feel wrong. Sometimes they are. Sometimes you're just used to doing it your way.

When you disagree: Ask it why. Not to argue — to understand its logic. Half the time you'll realize you left out crucial context. Half the time you'll catch yourself being stubborn.

When it's actually wrong: Your agent will hallucinate. Be confidently incorrect. That's not bad faith — it's just how it works. Catch it, correct it, move on. Your agent learns from corrections.

The First Week

Day 1-2: Have a real conversation. Share something you're stuck on. Let your agent surprise you.

Day 3-4: Iterate once. Tell your agent what worked and what didn't. See how it adjusts.

Day 5+: Do another small thing. You'll already have the rhythm.
