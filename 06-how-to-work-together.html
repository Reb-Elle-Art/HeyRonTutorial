How to Work Together

This is where the partnership actually happens.

You've got the mindset. You've got your setup. Now, how do you collaborate?

The Iteration Rhythm

You won't get it right the first time. That's not failure — that's how this works.

First draft: "Here's what I'm thinking. What do you build from this?"

Review: Read what it produces. Note what works and what doesn't.

Refinement: "Keep the [part that works]. Change [part that doesn't] because [reason]."

Repeat until done.

The key: Each iteration should move you closer to what you actually want. If it's moving sideways, reset. Tell your agent: "This isn't the direction I want. Let's try a different approach."

Questions That Get Results

Ask Your Agent to Think Out Loud

  • "Walk me through how you'd approach this."
  • "What are the trade-offs here?"
  • "What assumptions are you making?"

These force your agent to show its reasoning. Half the time, you'll catch an issue. The other half, you'll understand why the approach is solid.

Ask It to Challenge You

  • "What am I not considering?"
  • "Where could this break?"
  • "Is there a simpler way to do this?"

Your agent is often better at pattern-matching than you are. Use that.

Be Specific When Something's Off

Instead of: "That's not quite right."
Say: "This part doesn't fit because [reason]. Can you adjust for [specific constraint]?"

Vagueness requires guessing. Specifics are actionable.

Breaking Work Into Pieces

Don't hand your agent a giant project. Break it into pieces.

Instead of: "Build me a landing page."

Try: "I need a landing page for [thing]. Here's what's on it: [sections]. I want it to feel [tone]. Let's start with just the HTML structure and a basic stylesheet. Once that's done, we can iterate on styling."

Each chunk lets you:

  • See what it produces before moving forward
  • Give specific feedback
  • Adjust direction if needed
  • Build confidence in the collaboration

Disagreement Is Information

Your agent will suggest things you don't like. Sometimes that's taste. Sometimes that's insight.

If you hate it: Say why specifically. "This feels too corporate" beats "I don't like this."

If you're unsure: Ask it to explain the reasoning. Maybe you'll learn something. Maybe you'll confirm your instinct was right.

If it keeps missing the mark: It might not have enough context. Share more. Show examples. Point to things that work and ask why they work.

Knowing When to Ship

You can iterate forever. At some point, you take what you've got and ship it.

Ask yourself: "Is this good enough for what I need right now?"

If yes, done. If no, one more round of refinement. Then call it.

Your framework isn't final. You'll rebuild it. Your document isn't perfect. Neither is the competition's. Get it out.

When Something Breaks

If a process isn't working:

Tell your agent: "This process isn't working because [reason]. Can we try [different approach]?"

There's no shame in changing course — the shame is staying with a broken system because you feel committed to it.

Checkpoint

  • I understand the iteration rhythm
  • I can ask my agent specific, actionable questions
  • I know how to break work into pieces
  • I'm comfortable disagreeing and trying different approaches
  • I know when to ship vs. when to refine more
