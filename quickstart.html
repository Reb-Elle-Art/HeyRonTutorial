Module 01 — What You Got

Your agent arrives with context — a set of files, tools, and memories that define what it "knows" about your setup. Understanding this context is the first step to working together effectively.

But more importantly, this is where you start learning precision.

What the Agent Can See

Your agent has access to:

  • Project files — everything in the workspace folder
  • Memory files — curated long-term context (SOUL.md, USER.md, MEMORY.md)
  • Tool credentials — API keys and integrations you have configured
  • Session history — what you've discussed in current and recent chats

What It Can't See

  • Files outside the workspace
  • Your local browser, other open apps
  • Anything you haven't explicitly shared or stored in the workspace

Why Boundaries Matter

The limits of what your agent can see aren't restrictions. They're a feature.

When you know exactly what the agent knows, you start thinking differently about:

  • What to share — Not "give it everything" but "what does it actually need to know?"
  • How to organize — Files in your workspace become a form of communication with your agent (and your future self)
  • What's private — Some things don't go in the workspace. That boundary teaches you clarity.

Context Is Collapsible

You can choose how much context to load for any given task. The agent doesn't need to "know" everything all the time — you can point it at specific files or projects as needed.

This is the first precision practice: Give the agent exactly what it needs. Not more. Not less. This simple discipline teaches you to think clearly about what you actually want.

A Real Example

In a real workspace, you might have:

workspace/
├── SOUL.md              ← Who I am as an agent
├── USER.md              ← Who you are
├── MEMORY.md            ← Curated long-term wisdom
├── AGENTS.md            ← How we work together
├── memory/
│   ├── daily/           ← Session logs
│   ├── projects/        ← Project-specific notes
│   └── decisions/       ← Decisions and why
├── p-projects/          ← Your active projects
└── archive/             ← Finished work

The agent can see all of this. But notice what's not here: your email, your browser history, your financial documents. You chose the boundary. That choice says something about trust — and about what you're building together.

What This Teaches You

The first time you set this up, take your time. Walk through your workspace folder. Ask yourself:

  • What does the agent need to understand me?
  • What do I want to keep private?
  • How would I organize this differently if someone else could see it?

These questions aren't technical. They're about clarity. And clarity is where the real work begins.

The curiosity here: What emerges when you think carefully about what you're willing to share? What does that boundary reveal about how you want to work?
Module 02 — First Conversation

Your first session sets the tone for the entire partnership. But it's not about getting everything right. It's about showing up honestly.

Partner is a verb.

Here's What Partnering Actually Looks Like

  • You share context. Your agent doesn't know your project, constraints, preferences. You have to tell it. The more you share, the better it gets.
  • You ask it to show its work. When your agent gives you an answer, ask "how did you get there?" or "what other options did you consider?" This is how you learn how it thinks.
  • Ask it if you are missing anything. Use it to catch your blind spots and identify your bias.
  • You iterate together. The first response won't be right. You refine, adjust, get closer. That's the work.

Your First Real Conversation

Don't ask your agent to solve something. Ask it to explore something with you.

Bad: "Help me organize my notes."

Better: "I have 200 notes scattered across three folders. I work on multiple projects simultaneously. Some notes span multiple projects. I can't find anything, and it's slowing me down. What structure would actually help me?"

The second gives your agent the actual problem, not just the surface request. Include:

  • What you're trying to do
  • What you've already tried
  • What's broken about the current approach
  • Any constraints (time, tools, skill level)
  • What success looks like

Then Tell It About Yourself

Not your resume. The parts that shape how you work:

  • Your tempo: "I like to think through things slowly" or "I move fast and iterate"
  • Your style: "I prefer short answers unless I ask for more" or "Give me the full context"
  • What matters: "Accuracy is non-negotiable" or "Speed matters more than perfection"
  • Your technical level: "I know what CLI is, but not how to use it" or "I am brand new to technology"
  • What you're building: "I'm learning about X" or "I'm trying to solve Y"

These aren't constraints. They're invitations. They tell the agent who you are.

When It Gets Something Wrong

It will. Good. That's when the partnership actually starts.

Say, "Actually, I prefer X" or "That's not quite right — here's why." Watch how the agent responds.

If the agent apologizes profusely or gets defensive, tell it you don't want apologies — you want to move forward. Then ask: Where did it log that information? Did it update SOUL.md? If not, ask it to record this behavior. Memory only exists if the agent makes a file. You're learning the rhythm together.

Curiosity as Practice

Curiosity isn't just "being interested." It's a specific set of behaviors:

Ask your agent to read the manual for you. Paste a documentation URL and say: "Summarize what this does, then tell me how it applies to my situation. Assume I'm at this experience level." Your agent becomes a research partner.

Ask "What am I missing?" when you're stuck. Have your agent look at your setup and tell you what you haven't considered. Fresh pattern-matching on your context often reveals blind spots.

Ask it why. Half the time you'll realize you left out crucial context. Half the time you'll catch yourself being stubborn. When it's wrong: Catch it, correct it, move on. Your agent learns from corrections.

Ask it to explain. Follow the curiosity where it leads. When your agent mentions something interesting — a concept, a tool, a pattern — ask it to explain. You're not just solving today's problem; you're building your own understanding.

What Slowness Looks Like Here

Don't establish all the rules at once. Show up, do real work, correct as you go. Let the partnership define itself through actual interaction.

You're learning how you work with an agent. A system you invent now, before you know your rhythm, will need reimagining later. Start with the work. Let the system emerge from how you actually work together.

Module 03 — Workspace

The workspace is where your files live — and what your agent can see and modify. Keep it organized, and the agent can work effectively.

Simple Structure

You don't need complexity. A flat structure with descriptive names often works better than deep folders:

project/
  drafts/
  final/
  archive/

Naming Conventions

  • Use dashes for spaces: my-project-file.md
  • Dates as YYYY-MM-DD for sorting
  • Prefix with status: draft-, final-, archive-

What the Agent Can Access

Everything in the workspace folder. But be intentional — don't clutter it with files the agent doesn't need.

Tip: Keep a README.md in each project with a one-line description of what lives there.
Module 04 — Storage Strategy

Where things live matters. Your agent needs to know where to find persistent data and how to handle backups.

Memory Layers

  • Session memory: What's in the current chat
  • File memory: What's in the workspace
  • Curated memory: Long-term context (SOUL.md, USER.md, MEMORY.md)

Backups

Your agent can push to GitHub. Set up a backup repo and use it for:

  • End-of-session snapshots
  • Before major changes
  • Archive of completed projects
Consider granular tokens in GitHub, instead of classic, to separate personal repos from your backups.
Module 05 — Your Toolkit

Your agent comes with tools. Here's how to use them effectively.

Available Tools

  • AgentMail — Send/receive emails through your agent
  • Whisper — Voice-to-text for voice memos
  • here.now — Instant static web hosting
  • GitHub — Version control and backups
  • Cloudflare Worker — Custom backend workflows

Start with One

Don't enable everything at once. Pick one tool, master it, then add the next.

Pro tip: Each tool should have a clear purpose. If you can't explain why you need it, you probably don't.
Module 06 — How to Work Together

The best partnerships have rhythm. Here's how to find yours.

Commands vs. Conversations

  • Commands: Short, routine tasks. "Check my emails." "Publish this."
  • Conversations: New territory. "Here's what I'm trying to do..."

Feedback Loops

Correct concisely. Don't apologize for corrections — just state what changed:

"Actually, I prefer X approach. Let's do it that way."

Initiative

Decide: Does the agent propose unprompted, or only act when asked? There's no right answer — but be consistent.

Module 07 — Sample Project

A concrete example that puts everything together. Follow the steps and see the workflow in action.

Goal

Create a small static site, push it to a GitHub repo, and publish via here.now.

Steps

  1. Initialize a new folder my-sample with an index.html file.
  2. Write a short paragraph introducing the project.
  3. Run git init and add the file.
  4. Commit with a clear message.
  5. Push to the backup repo (confirm before push).
  6. Publish the folder with here.now (confirm).
  7. Review the live URL and note the wake you leave behind.
Tip: Use the same naming convention for folders and GitHub repos to keep the "wake" traceable.
Module 08 — Troubleshooting

When things go sideways, here's a systematic approach.

Common Issues

  • File not found: Check the path and ensure it's inside the workspace.
  • Permission denied: Verify file permissions and that the agent has read/write access.
  • Publish fails: Confirm the target directory is clean and no unsaved changes exist.

Diagnostic Steps

  1. Ask the agent to list the workspace contents.
  2. Check the .herenow/state.json for recent actions.
  3. Review the AGENTS.md and HEARTBEAT.md for any required context refresh.
If a step fails, ask for a short error report and then retry with stricter constraints.
